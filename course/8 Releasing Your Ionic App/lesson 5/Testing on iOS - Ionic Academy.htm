Testing on iOS

Releasing Your Ionic App

We want to upload our app to the iOS Testflight program, and as said before you need a Mac to perform the following steps. Basically, uploading a new version of your app to the iOS portal consists of a few steps which are the same most of the time:

  1. Set the version of your app local
  2. Build your app
  3. Archive your app from Xcode
  4. Upload your artifact (*.ipa) to the App Store
  5. Select your Build version for testing inside iTunes Connect

Letâs go through these steps, all of them are easy as we already have our accounts connected and everything is just waiting for us to start building the app!

First of all we need to set the bundle id, the app version and also the name of our app inside the config.xml at the root of our project. This file is used once we start the command line build from Ionic and these 3 values are essential for your app.

  • The bundle id has to match the ID you specified for your app inside the developer program before
  • The version should be a reasonable number and follow the schema MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH (e.g. 1.0.0)
  • The app name is the name that will be visible below your app icon after downloading it

This could for example look like this:

XHTML
1
2
<widget id="com.devdactic.book-ap" version="1.0.0">
<name>Devdactic</name>

Whenever you update your app you have to increase this version. Both iOS and Android are very picky that you can only upload versions with a higher code so be careful with your counting unless you want to be like Mozilla at version 40 soon.

Once your config is ready we can build the app, and we use the Ionic CLI for this so simply call:

ZSH
1
ionic build ios --release

This command will build our iOS native project inside platforms/ios which is now an Xcode project that you can simply open. Although you might have never used Xcode before (or plan to use it) we need it to easily submit our app directly to our account.

Remember, we already connected our Developer profile with Xcode so it will handle the rest for us in the background. Now go ahead and open the generated *.xcodeproj file which is the file that brings up the complete project in Xcode.

We donât need a complete Xcode introduction now as we just need a few functions. You can click around and inspect your project settings but donât glide off the path too far! We are here for one reason only!

At the top left you should see the name of your app and next to it a dropdown with devices. Select the Generic iOS Device and select Product -> Archive from the top menu.

This action will build an app artifact that is ready for upload, and this can take some time depending on the size of your project. Hopefully you see a message Build Succeeded soon and Xcode opens the screen below.

This organizer lists your projects to the left and the build artifacts in the center. The current build should be at the top, and you can also validate it from the time stamp.

We are now ready to hit the Upload to App Store button (what a magical moment) to send our created file to the iTunes connect platform.

Once you click that button, a little wizard guides you through some steps where we can stay at the default settings. If you are member of multiple iOS developer programs you might also be asked to select the correct profile to upload to.

After you have selected the correct account Xcode will ask about what to send to Apple. Leave the settings like they are in the image above and finally start the upload process.

If you have done everything so far correctly, from generating the app ID to building your Ionic app you will see this confirmation screen above which means you are now 99% done. This is a moment to celebrate.

You can now be sure that you have done everything correct local and your app was send to Apple. We can leave our environment now and focus on the iTunes Connect platform again, as this is the place to start Testflight.

If you havenât open iTunes connect, login and select your app like we did before. Select the Testflight slider at the top, then on the left hand side of the menu will be the entry Internal Testing like on the image below.

Internal testing means that you can test your app with people that are also part of your iOS Developer Program, so mostly internal people. External testing allows you to invite people just by their email, so everyone can be a tester then.

Inside the screen you can add testers, so I added myself as a tester with a different email. Make sure to either add your internal testers here or go to the External Testing screen and add the persons you would like to have as testers.

When you are done adding emails, itâs time to put our uploaded app version inside the test. Hit the Select Version to Test button and an overlay will appear that shows all of your previously uploaded builds.

Select the build you just compiled in Xcode and check that the time and Version is correct, then click next.

You have now moved a new version to Testflight, and if you want to upload a new version you need to perform the same steps as before from Xcode to iTunes connect. Your screen will now show that you have a selected build and you now have a new button Start Testing at the top right side.

Once you start the test, your testers will be automatically informed by Apple. They will get an email with a link to the app inside the Testflight app. The link will guide them to the App store if they have never used the app before, but if they ask you where to find it tell them to download the app!

So now people are able to test your app on their devices without anything else to do!

Finally, letâs take a look at the external testing. Most of the process is the same, but once you start an external test you also need to specify a few information for these testers like in the image below.

External testing most of the time means you either have people outside of your organization/company test the app, or even complete strangers if you for example put up an announcement of your app on a forum and invite people to be your Beta tester.

For all of these people itâs a good idea to let them know what they can currently do with your app as not all features might have been implemented yet.

If you want them to test some specific function this is also the place to instruct them. You could list a few use cases they should tests extensive and hopefully you will get some detailed feedback if something breaks.

If you are an internal or external tester, both will use the Testflight app and the final result they see inside that store will look like the image below (obviously you will have different information and graphics).

Itâs a good feeling to finally see the app there. If your app is approved for Testflight you can also be very confident that it will be allowed for the official App store as they might already have checked the app now.

Also, from experience the process of moving your app from Testflight stage to production is faster once itâs approved here.

We are done with iOS and have built and submitted the app to Testflight for internal and external people, letâs head over to Android now!
